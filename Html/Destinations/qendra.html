Skanderbeg Square

The Skanderbeg Square (Albanian: Sheshi Skënderbej) is the main plaza in the centre of Tirana, Albania.
The square is named after the Albanian national hero Gjergj Kastrioti Skënderbeu.

1923

In 1923, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana was a modest open space that served as the heart of the city's social and political life. The square, named after the national hero, George Kastrioti Skanderbeg, was surrounded by simple buildings, with limited development reflecting the early stages of Albania's modernization during the interwar period.

1943

In 1943, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana was heavily influenced by the Italian fascist occupation, with the square becoming a focal point for Italian military and political presence. The square's environment reflected the occupation, with architectural changes such as the construction of the fascist-era buildings, marking a shift from its previous Ottoman-style influence.

1951

In 1951, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana was undergoing significant changes as the city began to modernize under the socialist regime. The square became a symbol of the new communist identity, with monuments and architecture reflecting the ideological shift, including the removal of some fascist-era structures.

1963

In 1963, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana was a focal point of socialist architecture, with the construction of new public buildings reflecting the city’s industrial progress. The square hosted important political events, and the surrounding area became increasingly symbolic of the state's power and control over public life.

1988

In 1988, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana remained a key location for state-sponsored celebrations, with its monumental design symbolizing the strength of the socialist regime. The square was still dominated by large public buildings, but the city was beginning to show signs of change as Albania’s isolation from the rest of the world started to weaken.

2010

Former Tirana mayor Edi Rama embarked on a plan to modernize and Europeanize the square. In March 2010, works began to transform the square into a pedestrians and public transport only area. A new fountain would use rain water as its water supply, while a two-meter high pyramid would be built and leveled with a 2.5% slope throughout the square. During the construction period, detour roads have been put in place to gradually establish the new and permanent road in the ring road around the square. The entire project is funded by a grant from the State of Kuwait.

2011

The use of the square by all motor vehicles was restored through the construction of a narrower road segment around the center of the square including bicycle lanes. The existing green field south of Skanderbeg's statue was extended northward for a few hundred meters, while trees were planted in most places.

2016

The next mayor, Erion Veliaj, announced that instead the 2010 plan would be reintroduced with minor changes such as greater green space areas around the square, underground parking, and the introduction of stone material taken from all corners of Albania, and Albanian inhabited lands. Albania's rich flora would be represented in the gardens around the square, while the former garden behind Skanderbeg's monument would be restored to the pre-2010 state and named Europe Park.

2020

In 2020, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana was significantly impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, with public gatherings and events reduced to curb the spread of the virus. Despite the challenges, the square remained an important symbol of the city, and the surrounding area saw continued development and restoration projects aimed at revitalizing the city's center.

2022

In 2022, Skanderbeg Square in Tirana underwent further urban development, with new public spaces and modern amenities enhancing its accessibility and aesthetic appeal. The square also hosted several cultural events and became a central point for citizens to gather, reflecting the city’s ongoing efforts to blend history with contemporary urban life.

2025

Even now, Skanderbeg Square stands as a dynamic heart of Tirana, blending modern energy with rich history. The square has evolved into a lively cultural epicenter, with beautifully landscaped green areas, bustling cafés, and vibrant events that captivate both locals and visitors, making it a must-see destination in the city.
